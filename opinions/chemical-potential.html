What does chemical potential actually mean?

Taeyang Han  ·  Process Thermodynamics, Sogang University

April 2026 · 9 min read · Thermodynamics

Today, my professor covered the general framework of Solution Thermodynamics and introduced the concept of chemical potential, its definition and how it is useful in describing phase equilibrium condition. For such an important concept, the derivation is fairly straightforward but the implication it has in analysing the thermodynamics of a solution is anything but simple.

In this essay, I will attempt to explain what chemical potential actually means: why μᵢ behaves the way it does, what it means for two phases to share an equal chemical potential and how it differs in liquids and gases.

The definition, and what it actually says

The chemical potentialSymbol: μᵢ, Chemical potential is used to describe how much the total Gibbs Free energy of a system changes for every small amount of species i added into the system while holding T, P and all other species constant. of species i is defined as:

Definition of chemical potential
μᵢ ≡ [∂(nG) / ∂nᵢ]T, P, nⱼ

Reading this literally: if we add an infinitely small amount of species i to a mixture at fixed T and P, μᵢ tells us how much the total Gibbs Free energyG = H − TS. The Gibbs Free energy is the lowest at equilibrium for processes at constant T and P, which is why it is useful to analyse chemical and phase equilibria of a solution. changes per mole added. A large μᵢ means the system's energy rises sharply when more of species i enters. A small μᵢ means it enters at little energetic cost.

Why Gibbs Free energy and not the others?

The definition above uses G, but this is not the only way to define μᵢ. Chemical potential appears identically as the partial molar derivative of all four thermodynamic potentials — each holding different variables constant:

μᵢ from all four thermodynamic potentials
μᵢ = (∂U/∂nᵢ)S, V, nⱼ
μᵢ = (∂H/∂nᵢ)S, P, nⱼ
μᵢ = (∂A/∂nᵢ)V, T, nⱼ
μᵢ = (∂G/∂nᵢ)P, T, nⱼ

All these values give the same quantity. The reason the Gibbs energy version is used almost exclusively because of its direct relationship with temperature and pressure, which are easily controlled in every real process and laboratory experiment. Holding entropy or volume constant is mathematically convenient but experimentally difficult.

Why is it called the chemical "potential"?

The word "potential" is essentially the same potential that is used in gravitational potential energy. It refers to the energy that is 'stored' in a solution through the interactions between the species making up a system. Since systems at constant T and P tend to move toward a minimum Gibbs Free energy state (the equilibrium state), the chemical potential can be thought of as a measure of how much "potential energy" is stored in the system due to the presence of species i. This potential energy is a measure of where in the solution can species i move to reduce the total G. If μᵢ is high in one phase and low in another, species i will transfer spontaneously from the high-μ phase to the low-μ phase because that transfer lowers the total Gibbs energy of the system.

Another way to view this is to understand μᵢ as a driving force that moves species i in a solution from one phase to another, much like temperature and pressure:

Chemical potential and pressure

Since μ = G/n for a pure substance and dG = VdP − SdT, it follows directly that:

Pressure and temperature dependence of chemical potential
dμ = VdP − SdT

At constant temperature this reduces to (∂μ/∂P)T = V. The steepness of μ with pressure is set by the molar volume. For an incompressible substanceA liquid or solid where molar volume V barely changes with pressure. The integral of V dP from P° to P reduces to a simple linear term V(P − P°). — a liquid or solid where V is effectively constant — integrating at constant T gives:

Incompressible substance (liquid or solid)
μ = μ° + V(P − P°)

For a gas, this falls apart because V is not constant. Substituting V = RT/P for an ideal gas and integrating:

Ideal gas
μ = μ° + RT ln(P / P°)

The logarithm appears because ideal gas volume is inversely proportional to pressure and integrating 1/P over the pressure change gives ln(P). From the equation, we can see that the chemical potential is proportional to pressure: as pressure increases, μ increases.

What ln(P/P°) is actually saying

Finally, we can close the discussion on the ln(P/P°) term. This term reflects something important: chemical potential, unlike temperature or pressure, is not measured on an absolute scale. Only the differences between two phases matter. If the reference state μ° talks about the molecule's raw intrinsic energy at standard conditions then the RT ln(P/P°) term captures how much the actual state deviates from that reference.

This is why chemical potential is harder to build intuition for than temperature. You cannot point to a number and say "the chemical potential is −3200 J/mol, therefore..." in the way you can say "it's 80°C." What you can say is whether two phases have equal μᵢ — and that equality is the condition that governs whether a system will stay put or change. Every VLE calculation, every phase diagram, every activity coefficient model is ultimately solving for the conditions under which that equality holds.

The question is less of how much is the value of μᵢ and rather how much is the difference of μᵢ between two phases. Only through this comparison can we determine the direction of mass transfer and whether the system is at equilibrium.

TL;DR — Chemical Potential

Summary

μᵢ is a single number that answers one question: does species i want to move? It is defined as how much the total Gibbs Free energy of a mixture changes when you add a tiny amount of species i at fixed T and P. High μᵢ means adding more costs energy (the species is under thermodynamic pressure to leave) and low μᵢ means the opposite.

All four thermodynamic potentials give the same μᵢ when differentiated with respect to nᵢ. The G-based definition is generally preferred because constant T and P are easily obtainable in real processes.

Through the relationship between chemical potential and pressure at constant T, we can find that the chemical potential of incompressible substances (liquids, solids) shift linearly with change in pressure — μ = μ° + V(P − P°). Though, the same idea does not apply to gases due to its change in volume. Chemical potential of ideal gases shift logarithmically with change in P — μ = μ° + RT ln(P/P°) — because gas volume is inversely proportional to pressure.

Finally, phase equilibrium is reached when μᵢ is equal in every phase. That equality is the starting point for every VLE, LLE and SLE calculation in chemical engineering.
